2. Model Collapse as Medium
Shumailov et al. (2024) demonstrated mathematically that models trained recursively on AI-generated data degenerate — tails of the distribution disappear, outputs converge to an ever-narrower mean. They called it "the curse of recursion." An artist who deliberately feeds a model its own outputs and documents the degeneration is not producing glitch but entropy — the system consuming itself. This is the long-form failure mode, failure as process rather than event.


Misalignment ranges from the local (a corrupted weight) to the systemic (a degenerating lineage). Each failure reveals a different layer of what the optimization was hiding.

Rather than polishing away artefacts, creators often seek them. Drawing on procedural structures, geometric deformations, AI misalignments and temporal distortions, this talk shows how systems and algorithms can benefit from controlled instability. It explores the artistic value of computational “misbehaviour,” demonstrating how elements traditionally treated as flaws, such as glitches or hallucinations, become deliberate materials for expression. Situated at the intersection of art, generative modelling, and generative AI, the talk highlights how these dynamics enable compelling, reflective, and at times stunning audiovisual experiences.

1. The optimization trap 
Core question: What does a generative system optimize for, and what gets expelled in the process?
Frame the two dominant camps from the anti-ai art manifesto: those who reject generative AI outright, and those who enthusiastically chase ever-more-realistic, ever-more-"aesthetically pleasing" outputs. Both camps treat failure as a defect to be corrected. This talk proposes a third position: failure is the primary material.

Opening image: a side-by-side of a photorealistic Midjourney portrait versus Klingemann's Neural Glitch output, same formal vocabulary, entirely different artistic territory.

2. A genealogy: from accident to medium
Failure as artistic strategy predates generative AI by decades. Establish the intellectual lineage so the AI work doesn't appear context-free.


Ceramics and material process: Kintsugi 
Man Ray's Rayographs (1920s) 
Jackson Pollock & Francis Bacon
Auguste Rodin, left seam lines from his casting 
Monotype printing — a plate is inked, printed once, and the residual ghost impression is then re-printed, producing an image that is structurally a trace of a trace. The degradation is the technique. Edgar Degas was a master of this, using the ghost prints as finished works.
Samuel Beckett is the strongest case: Waiting for Godot and Endgame are structurally constituted by failure — repetition, non-arrival, broken machinery of dramatic convention. The plays do not fail to deliver plot; they make the failure of plot their subject. "Try again. Fail again. Fail better." is not just an aphorism but a description of the compositional method.
John Cage's 4'33" reframes ambient noise — which classical concert practice treats as failure — as the actual content. But the closer analog to glitch aesthetics might be Aleatoric composition more broadly (Cage, Stockhausen, Christian Wolff): the score designs conditions under which performers generate unpredictable outcomes, removing the composer from authorship of the specific result.

 -> Transition to digital art

Rosa Menkman, Glitch Studies Manifesto (2010) + The Collapse of PAL: "the dominant, continuing search for a noiseless channel has been — and will always be — no more than a regrettable, ill-fated dogma." The move from accidental glitch to deliberate glitch to glitch as vocabulary
Key conceptual move: the glitch does not just interrupt the image — it reveals the architecture of the medium. This is what makes it analytically useful, not merely decorative.

Take-home for this section: the question is never whether to use failure, but whether the artist controls which failure modes to invite.

-> What are possible parameter / domains to introduce misbehavior?

3. Geometric deformations: controlled instability (10 min)
Before neural networks, procedural and physical simulation already demonstrated how instability could be choreographed.

Quayola, Strata / Jardins d'Été (ongoing): photographic references dissolved into force-based geometric fields — the simulation is always one parameter away from collapse, and that proximity is the aesthetic
Memo Akten, Learning to See (2017–ongoing): a neural network trained on deliberately narrow datasets — fire, water, plants — hallucinates entire landscapes onto any input. The limitation of the training data is the expressive device. Everything looks like it's becoming something else.

Key concept for this section: controlled instability — the artist does not produce the image; the artist designs the conditions under which the system finds its own failure modes. Connects to your Lecture 2 dimension of WHO (the system as co-author of its own anomalies).

4. Neural misbehaviour: misalignment as medium (12 min)
The richest territory, and the section most directly responding to the anti-ai manifesto.

Mario Klingemann, Neural Glitch (2018–ongoing): deliberately corrupting trained GAN weights — "the same input can yield very different results depending on the glitch." The model misinterprets its own training on both texture and semantic levels. What emerges are "glimpses of autonomous creativity." Direct connection to the manifesto's call to explore "uncharted territories."
Sofia Crespo / Entangled Others, Neural Zoo / Beneath the Neural Waves: models trained on biological data that hallucinate organisms that could not biologically exist. Crespo reads the model's misclassification as generative — the network invents speculative life forms. The "failure" to accurately reproduce becomes a form of speculative biology.
Jake Elwes, Zizi — Queering the Dataset (2019) / Zizi in Motion (2023, V&A): injecting 1,000 drag identity images into a 70,000-face normative facial recognition dataset causes the model to visibly break down — mascara dissolves into lipstick, faces smear across gender. The failure is politically charged: the model's normativity is made legible precisely by its misbehaviour. "Zizi is lossy, the continuity of the original human performers is unrecoverable."

This section's central argument: these are not failures of tools — they are failures of the optimization target, which is precisely the point. The anti-ai manifesto frames this as defeating "the feedback loop that optimizes for a bland, neutered reinforcement of a narrow perspective of how 'aesthetically pleasing' a generated artifact should be."

5. Temporal distortions: time as failure axis (8 min)
Failure becomes most visible along the temporal dimension — where computational artifacts smear, loop, or drift.

Nur kurz:
Takeshi Murata, Monster Movie (2005): early datamoshing — video compression algorithms exploited so that pixel data from one frame bleeds into the motion vectors of another. Time becomes plastic.
Rosa Menkman, Collapse of PAL: compression artifacts as temporal archaeology — the decay reveals the encoding protocol
Recent video diffusion: current video generation models frequently produce temporal incoherence — flickering geometry, morphing faces — which artists working in this space (e.g. contributors to the anti-ai manifesto ecosystem) are actively embracing as an aesthetic signature rather than correcting
Optional: Ian Cheng, Emissaries (2015–2017): live simulation systems "allowed to drift" — the work is constitutively unfinished and the artist cannot predict what will happen

Key concept: time is the axis on which the architecture of a computational system becomes perceptible. Temporal artifacts are not defects in the image; they are evidence of how the system encodes change.

6. The antagonism argument: designing against the optimization (8 min)
This is where the uploaded paper enters.
Draw the parallel between Cai, Arawjo & Glassman's "sycophantic AI" in interaction design and the equivalent in aesthetics: both RLHF for pleasantness and RLHF for photorealism optimize away from the interesting. The anti-ai manifesto and the antagonistic AI paper share a "shadow paradigm" logic — both argue for deliberately inverting the default optimization target.
The manifesto's most elegant insight: if anti-ai aesthetics become culturally relevant enough to enter training datasets, they corrupt the models from within — "leading to their own deterioration." The misbehaviour is self-propagating.
But introduce the important caveat the Antagonistic AI paper provides: antagonism without intentionality is just noise. The paper's three conditions — consent, context, framing — translate directly to artistic practice: the artist must design the conditions under which misbehaviour is invited (framing), must understand what failure mode is specific to this system (context), and must take authorial responsibility for the outcome (consent/intentionality). Random glitch is not art; chosen glitch is.

7. Closing: a poetics of malfunction (7 min)
Who authors the failure?
The artist as a designer of conditions rather than producer of outcomes. This connects directly to your broader research theme of pipeline authorship and "The Algorithmic Protagonist" — a protagonist is interesting precisely because it can deviate, surprise, resist.
The most generative aesthetic territory in computational art is not where systems work as designed, but where they work as undesigned — in the gap between what the training data anticipated and what the model was asked to do, in the frequencies outside the latent space that commercial optimizers scrub clean.
Take-home: Failure is not the absence of craft. It is a craft vocabulary that only becomes available when you stop asking the system to reproduce the world and start asking it to reveal itself.
