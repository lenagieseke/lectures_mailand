Lecture and workshop invite students to consider computation not as a tool to master, but as a material to think and create with. What processes, aesthetics, and forms of authorship does code offer as a medium, and how does it shape contemporary artistic and design practice? In turn, how does artistic thinking shape code, and how can programming be positioned as a space for exploration, experimentation, and expression?

## Concept Developing

"Against the Grain"
Storyline: Each section of the agenda is reframed as a site of resistance. Instructions can be misused. Algorithms can be broken or inverted. Systems can be pushed into collapse. GenAI can be hallucinated, poisoned, or refused. The design principles section then becomes not a how-to but a manifesto — a vocabulary for productive misuse. The teasers could each be a canonical example of an artist who found their voice precisely by breaking the expected behavior of their medium.
Takehome: The artistic potential of code is not in its power to execute correctly, but in what it reveals when it fails, resists, or misbehaves — and the artist's job is to engineer those conditions deliberately.

A few structural notes: this framing makes the teaser slots very natural — three artists or works where "wrong" use produced something aesthetically or conceptually irreducible (e.g. Vera Molnár's systematic rule-breaking, Rosa Menkman's glitch manifesto, or even early Ulises Carrión as a proto-coder thinking against the book). It also gives you a clean rhetorical move at the GenAI section: if even the most opaque, seemingly unbreakable system can be subverted, then artistic agency is not foreclosed — it just requires more cunning.

From Tool to Material to Desired Friction

Conceptual arc
* The talk begins with the conventional view of code as a tool: Tools execute instructions. They help produce predictable results. This is how most software is introduced and marketed.
* Then the perspective shifts. When we actually program, we do not simply execute commands. We create systems with behavior. Loops, randomness, and feedback produce outcomes that were not individually designed.
At this point code begins to resemble a material rather than a tool. Materials have properties and tendencies. They generate textures, patterns, and constraints.
* Once a medium is understood as material, a classic artistic move appears: pushing it beyond its intended use. Artists start probing where the system fails, drifts, or produces unexpected results.
Those moments reveal the structure of the medium.
  
Examples naturally follow the technological stack:
* algorithmic repetition producing emergent patterns
* randomness producing variation beyond the designer’s control
* glitches revealing compression and encoding processes
* generative AI failures exposing statistical representation

Failure stops being a bug to eliminate. It becomes a method for understanding the medium.


Take-home
* These days, programming becomes interesting as an artistic medium precisely when it stops behaving like a reliable tool
* Creative coding treats computation as a material whose limits, frictions, and misbehaviors can become aesthetic form.

> Engineers try to make systems behave correctly.
> Artists discover what a medium is by seeing how it behaves when it doesn’t.


Instructions → Algorithms → Computational Systems → Generative AI → Design principles.
Each stage reveals more autonomy and more potential for misbehavior.

There is also a pleasant historical symmetry here. Early computer art in the 1960s already followed this logic. Plotters were built for precision engineering drawings. Artists immediately used them to explore randomness, irregularity, and generative variation. The machine was designed for control. The art emerged from letting it drift. A small reminder that every technological system secretly contains aesthetic accidents waiting to be discovered.


## EAiCCI

When new technology meets art, history shows that the consequences are never linear, they reorganise aesthetics, creative labour, rights, and markets,. Photography is a telling precedent. Early critics warned photography would corrupt or replace art (“If photography is allowed to supplement art in some of its functions, it will soon supplant or corrupt it altogether, thanks to the stupidity of the multitude which is its natural ally.”, Charles Baudelaire 1859)2,, and painters declared painting dead on first seeing a photograph (“This is the end of Art. I am glad I have had my day.”, often attributed to J. M. W. Turner 1839),. Portrait markets shifted and painters lost income (“One knows that photography has harmed painting considerably, and has killed portraiture especially, once the livelihood of the artist.”, Henri Le Secq 1863)2,. Painters then turned from realism toward perception and abstraction, developing movements including Impressionism and Symbolism, and argued that art is not mere imitation (“I have no fear of photography as long as it cannot be used in heaven and in hell. ... I am going to paint people who breathe, feel, love, and suffer.”, Edvard Munch)2,. This shift enriched the arts by expanding the visual vocabulary and opening new avenues of expression that shaped modern art. Comparable distributional conflicts recur across media history, from campaigns against recorded music (Figure 15,) to writers’ strikes linked to technological shifts in syndication (1977), pay television (1981), home video (1985), digital distribution (2007), streaming and AI (2023).,
Against this backdrop of recurring distributional conflicts, a further risk is the emergence of “QWERTY moments” where convenience and early market power lock in standards that are not artistically optimal or socially fair. In photography, 35 mm prevailed for portability and cost, despite arguments that larger formats offered richer tonal capacities. Edward Weston captured the necessity of artists adapting to their tools when he remarked that “Hence the photographer's most important and likewise most difficult task [...] is, learning to see his subject matter in terms of the capacities of his tools and processes, [...].” Such path dependence continues to shape practice, markets and taste long after the original constraints fade. Today, AI sits at a similar crossroads for Europe’s Cultural and Creative Industries. The open problem is how to guide early choices in data, rights, interfaces, and business models, so that AI QWERTY moments do not harden into unregulated and unfair disruption, and how to equip artists and CCIs to adopt AI on their terms, while safeguarding European values of cultural diversity, fairness, and sustainability.

"Misuse as Method"
Why: Brera design students are trained to interrogate function — it is the core disciplinary reflex. "Method" lands in that register immediately, positioning the talk not as a coding tutorial but as an epistemological proposal. It also rhymes with research methodology, which graduate-level design programs take seriously. And it is short enough to be a slogan they leave with.
  
Storyline: Open with the provocation that every design tool encodes assumptions about correct use — and that accepting those assumptions uncritically means designing for the tool rather than with it. Then move through the agenda as an escalating series of misuse strategies: instructions subverted, algorithms inverted, systems pushed to collapse, GenAI models coaxed into their blind spots. Each section pairs a canonical misuse example with a design principle extracted from it. The closing argument is that disobedience toward your medium is not ignorance — it is exactly how new design vocabularies get invented. 
  
Takehome: Understanding how a system is supposed to work is only the prerequisite. The design work begins when you ask what happens if it does not — and build that question into your practice.
