This talk offers an introduction to virtual production and its toolset. It outlines how real-time engines, LED volumes, performance capture and in-camera visual effects reshape the relationship between physical sets and digital environments. Through practical examples, the talk considers the creative and organisational implications of virtual production, showing how these techniques influence aesthetic and narrative decisions in contemporary filmmaking.

1. Framing the Problem
* Tension between physical and digital environments in narrative filmmaking
* Where traditional VFX pipelines fail
* Visual examples: greenscreen artefacts, compositing tells, actor disorientation (The Batman, Superman, The Mandalorian)

2. What is Virtual Production?
* Broad definition: the collapse of pre-, principal, and post-production into a continuous pipeline
* The VP toolset across production phases: previs / techvis / stuntvis -> mocap / facial capture / ICVFX -> * postvis / VFX-vis
* Key distinction: VP as workflow philosophy, not a single technology
* Content types on the LED wall: 2D, 2.5D (parallax layers), full 3D 

3. Core Technologies
3a. Real-Time Engines
* From game engines to cinematic tools: Unreal Engine nDisplay, Unity, Pixera, disguise
* Why real-time rendering matters on set: immediate feedback, iterative creative decisions, reduced post dependency
* The full ICVFX signal chain: camera tracking -> nDisplay nodes -> LED processors 

3b. Performance Capture and Greenscreen VP
* Mocap and facial capture within the VP pipeline
* Hybrid greenscreen VP: when and why greenscreen remains relevant alongside LED
* INTERCONNECTION (Film University Babelsberg, 2025) as case study

3c. LED Volumes
* Physical and optical properties: pixel pitch, brightness (nits), colour gamut, panel modularity
* How the wall functions as a light source: interactive lighting, elimination of greenscreen spill
* 2D, 2.5D, and 3D content strategies on the wall
* Limitations: moiré, limited field of view for parallax, high infrastructure cost, the "golden cage" problem * for camera movement

4. LED as Narrative Device — Beyond the Backdrop
* The dominant use case (and its problem): LED wall as an expensive greenscreen substitute, aesthetically inert
* Reframing the question: what can the wall do that no other medium can?
* Case study — INTERCONNECTION

5. Implications
* How VP changes the director–cinematographer–production designer relationship: decisions once deferred to post must be made collectively in prep
* Actor experience and performance: the argument for immersion
* The question of cinematic texture: does VP homogenise "gamey" style?
* Discarded ideas from INTERCONNECTION as productive failures: real-time motion capture and live interaction with digital characters — technically feasible in isolation, not yet stable enough for narrative production

6. Outlook
* Near-term: AI-assisted 3D content generation for LED backgrounds, transparent LED panels, seamless floor-to-wall volumes
* The shift from tool adoption to workflow integration: the field needs codified pipelines, not just hardware upgrades
