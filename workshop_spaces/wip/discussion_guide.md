# Final Workshop Discussion

Reflection → comparison → abstraction → big insight.

## 1. Reflection on the Design Process

Goal: make students aware of the **hidden decisions in translation**.

Prompt:
* When you designed your pipeline, what did you have to decide that was not obvious at first?

Follow-ups if the room is quiet:
* What did you have to simplify about the original phenomenon?
* What did you choose to measure, and what did you ignore?
* Did anyone change their idea because the capture method forced them to?

Useful framing:
* Every capture method reduces reality. What did your system throw away?

## 2. Discuss the Power of the Model

Goal: highlight that **the model defines the world**, not the source.

Prompt:
* Looking at the examples today, which step in the pipeline had the biggest impact on the final world?

Possible follow-ups:
* If you kept the same source but changed the model, how different would the world become?
* Did anyone choose a transformation rule mainly because it looked interesting visually?
* Did any group discover that a small rule created unexpected behavior?

Key insight to guide toward:
* The model determines what kinds of behavior can exist in the system.


## 3. Discuss the Third Space

Goal: connect their projects to the **structure–influence–meaning framework**.

Prompt:
* Which projects produced a system that could evolve or surprise you?

Follow-ups:
* Which systems were mostly reactive, and which were generative?
* Where did participants actually influence the system?
* Which projects had the clearest conceptual meaning?

If needed, contrast examples:
* particle ecology (strong system behavior, weaker meaning)
* attention economy example (strong conceptual meaning)

Key question:
* When does a translation become a world rather than just an effect?


## 4. Reflect on Tools and Constraints

Goal: expose how **technology shapes design choices**.

Prompt:
* Where did the tools or sensors constrain your ideas?

Follow-ups:
* Did anyone simplify their idea because the capture method was too complex?
* What technologies would you need to build your system for real?
* Would the concept change if the sensing technology changed?

Key insight:
* Tools do not just implement ideas — they shape them.


## 5. Step Back to the Big Idea: World-Building

Goal: connect the exercise to **broader computational design**.

Prompt:
* If computational systems create worlds, what responsibilities come with that?

Follow-ups:
* What does your system allow people to do?
* What behaviors does it encourage or discourage?
* What assumptions about people or the world does your system encode?

Example reflection prompt:
* Imagine your system existed in public space. What behaviors would it reward?


## 6. Closing Question (Final Reflection)

* After today, do you think designing computational systems is closer to engineering, art, or world-building?


> Every time we design a pipeline, we decide what can exist, how it behaves, and how people interact with it. That is why translational media is a form of world-building.
